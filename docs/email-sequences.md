## Welcome series (5 emails, ~5 weeks)

**Trigger:** newsletter signup form on the website (any page).
**Goal:** turn a curious one-time subscriber into a recurring monthly donor, earning the ask through four story-driven emails before requesting in email 5.
**Exit:** if the subscriber becomes a recurring donor at any point, remove from the series and enroll them in a future "recurring donor" cadence (out of scope for v1).

## Email 1 — Day 0

**Subject:** Welcome. Here is what your inbox just signed up for.
**Preview:** A letter from Romania, four times a year. Nothing more.

Hello,

Thank you for putting your name on our list. I am the board chair of Good Samaritan International, a 501(c)(3) we started in 1994 with my parents Nistor and Floare. The work has grown but the way it works has not changed. We walk with families in Romania by name, the board visits in person, and we pay for our own travel out of pocket.

Here is what you will hear from us:

A letter from Romania, four times a year. Sometimes a fifth if something urgent happens.

That is all. No banner sales. No pleas with countdown timers. No tracking pixels that report when you open this. We committed to that on our privacy page and we mean it.

If you would like a fuller picture of who we walked with this past year, the 2024 annual report names every family and breaks down every dollar.

Apostle Paul wrote that the apostles "asked only that we should continue to remember the poor, the very thing I was eager to do." That is the work.

Grateful for you,
The board

**CTA:** Read the 2024 annual report → `https://goodsamaritaninternational.org/annual-reports/2024.pdf`

**Why this email works:** Sets cadence expectations honestly (four times/year, not weekly), reaffirms the no-tracking promise from the privacy page, anchors with the Galatians 2:10 verse that's already used on the site. Avoids the "Welcome to our community!" cliché Margaret-the-donor recognizes as fake.

## Email 2 — Day 3

**Subject:** Marta Lutac is in treatment. Her grandson Bogdan needed two eye surgeries.
**Preview:** One family in Rauseni, Botosani county, this year.

Hello,

A few days ago you signed up to hear from us. I want to tell you about the family the board prayed with this past summer.

Marta Lutac is in cancer treatment in Rauseni, a village in Botosani county in northeast Romania. Her grandson Bogdan needed two eye surgeries this year. Romania's public health system covers the basics, but a single trip to a specialty hospital from rural Botosani can cost a family a week of groceries.

We covered Marta's medicine and the transport for both of them. We also sat with the family in their kitchen and prayed.

The cost to us was a small share of what we sent to medical needs in 2024. A hundred dollars covered the medicine for one round of Marta's treatment. Fifty dollars covered one trip for Bogdan's follow-up.

The Lutac family knows we are coming back. That is the part the dollar figure does not capture.

If you would like to read about the other families we walked with this year, the 2024 report has the full list.

Grateful for you,
The board

**CTA:** See our 2024 medical work → `https://goodsamaritaninternational.org/projects/medical`

**Why this email works:** Named-family specificity (Marta, Bogdan, village, county) builds the trust that lets the rest of the sequence ask anything. Dollar amounts ($100 and $50) ground the abstract idea of "medical aid" in real, household-scale numbers donors can match to their own grocery bill.

## Email 3 — Day 10

**Subject:** Why we pay our own travel.
**Preview:** The board model that keeps 98¢ of every dollar in the field.

Hello,

A donor once asked me why our annual report shows admin overhead at 2%. The honest answer is that the board has worked this way since 1994 and we have not seen a reason to change it.

Every board member is a volunteer. Every flight to Romania comes out of our own pockets. When we drive across Botosani or Brasov counties to visit families, the gas and lodging are on us. None of that touches your gift.

That is how 98 cents of every dollar reaches the field.

The other two cents covers genuine costs of being a US 501(c)(3): bank fees, the small portion of card-processing that Stripe takes, the IRS filings, and the printing of the annual report. We could probably trim further, but the work of accountability is worth that 2%.

If you want to see exactly how 2024 broke down across categories, the use-of-funds chart on our About page shows it. Fifty-six percent to needy families. Twenty percent to missionary support. Fourteen percent to housing. Six percent to aid delivery. Two percent to scholarships. Two percent to admin.

Grateful for you,
The board

**CTA:** See the 2024 use-of-funds breakdown → `https://goodsamaritaninternational.org/about`

**Why this email works:** Addresses the single biggest objection in the donor demographic (overhead) head-on, with the strongest fact in the arsenal (board pays own travel) from the annual report. The donor-asked-me framing is more credible than "we're proud of our 2% overhead."

## Email 4 — Day 21

**Subject:** How a dollar gets from a donor in Michigan to firewood in Avrameni.
**Preview:** The chain, in five steps.

Hello,

Last autumn the Ciobanasu family in Avrameni and the Murariu family across the border in Moldova received firewood and winter supplies before the snow came in. I thought you might want to know how that actually works.

1. A donor in the United States sends a gift, often through Zelle to gsworldoutreach@gmail.com. (Zelle is preferred because it has no card fees.)
2. The board chair logs the gift, tags it by fund (Housing, Medical, Firewood and winter, and so on), and sends a receipt.
3. In early autumn the board pre-orders firewood from a Romanian supplier we have worked with for years.
4. Local Romanian pastors compile a list of families on the edge in October. Bills go directly to the utility company; firewood is delivered to the family.
5. The next board visit in summer includes a stop at every family on the list, with photos and a per-family log in the next annual report.

Nothing in that chain involves cash changing hands in a way we cannot trace.

The Ciobanasu and Murariu families are warm this winter because someone in the US decided in September that fifty dollars was worth less to them than a month of heat for one family in rural Romania.

Grateful for you,
The board

**CTA:** Read about firewood and winter aid → `https://goodsamaritaninternational.org/projects/firewood`

**Why this email works:** Transparency narrative that walks the donor's dollar from their bank app to a specific Romanian village. The most powerful conversion move for accountability-conscious older donors; almost no other nonprofit publishes this chain in plain language.

## Email 5 — Day 35

**Subject:** What twenty-five dollars a month does, in twelve months.
**Preview:** A small ask, with a specific picture of what it adds up to.

Hello,

Over the past few weeks you have heard about the Lutac family in Rauseni, the way we keep 98 cents of every dollar in the field, and the chain that gets firewood to Avrameni in autumn. Thank you for reading along.

If our work resonates with you, here is what I would ask.

Twenty-five dollars a month becomes three hundred dollars over a year. That is the cost of insulating one wall of a Romanian home before winter, the way we helped the Anton family in Comana de Sus last year. Or a month of medicine for one round of cancer treatment. Or twelve weeks of groceries for an elderly widow whose pension does not stretch to the end of the month.

Fifty a month becomes a winter's heat for one family. One hundred a month becomes a small project on its own.

Monthly giving is the most useful pattern for us because it lets the board commit ahead of time to families who reach out in February or March, before our autumn donation surge.

Whatever you choose, you can cancel any time in one click from any Givebutter receipt.

Grateful for you,
The board

P.S. If monthly is not the right fit, a one-time gift or a Zelle to gsworldoutreach@gmail.com works the same way for us.

**CTA:** Set up monthly giving → `https://goodsamaritaninternational.org/donate`

**Why this email works:** Earns the ask through four prior emails of story + transparency. Every dollar amount anchors to a specific 2024 outcome (Anton family wall, Marta's medicine, an elderly widow's groceries). The "cancel anytime in one click" line surfaces a Margaret-persona-grade trust signal upfront. The P.S. offers an escape hatch so the email doesn't read as a hard close.

- Subscriber becomes a recurring donor → remove from welcome series,

## A/B test suggestions

1. **Email 5 subject line.** Test "What twenty-five dollars a month does, in twelve months." against "Would you walk with the Antons next?" Split 50/50 by send. The first is more analytical; the second is more relational. The winner reveals whether the audience converts more on math or on personal story.
2. **Email 2 send time.** Test Tuesday 10 a.m. local against Sunday 6 p.m. local. Sunday-evening after church is a strong slot for this audience but Tuesday morning catches the inbox while it's still empty. Split 50/50.
3. **Email 3 length.** Test the 220-word version above against a 130-word shorter cut that opens directly with "Every board member is a volunteer." See whether the donor-asked-me framing earns the extra read time.

Run one test at a time. Wait three weeks between tests so each has a full sequence cycle to measure.
